Artificial Intelligence

What is AI?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) simulates human intelligence with machines, especially computer systems. This includes learning (the collection of information and rules for using the information), the conclusion (using the rules to draw approximate or definitive conclusions) and self-correction. Special applications of AI are expert systems, speech recognition and machine vision. The term artificial intelligence (AI) was invented by John McCarthy, an American computer scientist, at the Dartmouth Conference in 1956. Today, AI is a generic term that ranges from robotic process automation (RPA) to actual robotics. Artificial intelligence has become increasingly important recently, in part due to Big Data and the increase in the speed, size, and variety of data that companies collect today. For example, AI can recognize patterns in data more efficiently than humans, giving companies more insight into their data. There are two types of AI’s. The first type is the weak AI. This is an AI system that is developed and trained for a specific task. Virtual personal assistants, like Siri from Apple, are a form of weak AI. The second type is the strong AI. The strong AI, also known as general artificial intelligence, is an AI system with generalized human cognitive skills so that when it get faced with an unknown task, it has enough intelligence to find a solution. The Turing test, developed Alan Turing in 1950, is a method to determine whether a computer can think like a human, but the method is controversial.

History

1950 TURING TEST

Computer scientist Alan Turing proposes a test for machine intelligence. If a machine can trick humans into thinking it is human, then it has intelligence. He thought that at year 2000 the average user has a 70% Chance to identify if it is a human or a machine. His prediction was incorrect.

1956 A.I. BORN

The Dartmouth Conference in summer 1956 in Hanover (New Hampshire) at the Dartmouth College was a 6 weeklong workshop. This workshop was the Birthplace of the term “Artificial Intelligence”.

1961 UNIMATE

Unimate was the first industrial robot. Unimate was first used by US automaker General Motors (GM) in 1961 and it revolutionized the industrial production with that. From the very beginning, the goal was flexible and universal automation.

1966 ELIZA

Eliza is a Pioneering chatbot developed by Joseph Weizenbaum. Eliza holds conversations with humans by using a simple “pattern matching” and substitution methodology.

1966 SHAKEY

Shakey was the first mobile robot who was able to reason about his own actions. While other robots would have to be instructed on each individual step of completing a larger task, Shakey could analyze commands and break them down into basic chunks by itself.

1996 DEEP BLUE

Deep Blue was a chess-playing computer from IBM. He lost the first match against the world chess champion Garry Kasparov with a score of 4-2. He was then heavily upgraded and played again versus Kasparov in 1997. He defeated Garry Kasparov with a score of 3 1/2 – 2 ½.

1998 KISMET

Kismet is a humanoid robot from the 1990s. It comes from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and consists of a head with a neck area. The robot was a popular object for a wide range of media and gained international fame. Research with Kismet was discontinued in 2000 and since then he has been an exhibit in the MIT Museum.

1999 AIBO

Aibo is an entertainment robot developed by the Japanese electronics company Sony as a toy. With the Aibo, Sony pioneered the introduction of robots into households. A total of more than 150,000 Aibos were sold. Aibo perceives information about its surroundings using a camera, microphones and, to a limited extent, tactile feedback. This information is processed in an internal computer. The output of this computer consists of an imitation of typical dog behavior, such as four-legged gait, non-verbal communication through movement of the ears and tail, rolling on the floor, etc. In addition, there is a sound or speech output and LEDs that directly display the simulated “mood” of the device.

2002 ROOMBA

Roomba is a vacuum cleaner robot series from iRobot with several models. The models belong to the service robots as vacuum cleaning robots. Roomba was launched in 2002 and sold over 15 million copies worldwide by 2016. Roomba is the world's most successful service robot.

2011 SIRI

Siri is software from Apple that is used to recognize, and process naturally spoken language and is intended to perform the functions of a personal assistant. Siri runs on Apple's operating systems iOS, macOS, watchOS and tvOS. At the end of 2016, Apple's servers processed around 2 billion Siri requests per week. Siri was initially only available on the iPhone 4s under iOS 5. With iOS 6, the assistant was also usable on iPads of the 3rd generation and newer, the iPhone 5 and finally the iPad mini and the iPod touch of the 5th generation. On September 9, 2014, the first Apple Watch with Siri support was announced. Exactly one year later, the 4th generation Apple TV was released, which also supports Siri. With the release of macOS Sierra, the voice assistant also found its way into the personal computer product lines.

2011 WATSON

IBM’s question answering computer Watson wins first place on popular $1M prize television quiz show Jeopardy. To demonstrate its capabilities, the program competed in three episodes of the quiz show Jeopardy! with two human opponents who had previously won record sums on the show. The game, for which a million-dollar prize was awarded, was therefore compared in the media with the duel of chess world champion Garri Kasparov against the computer Deep Blue.The system won the game with a final score of $ 77,147 over the $ 24,000 and $ 21,600 of its human rivals.In January 2017, a Japanese insurance company replaced more than 30 employees with the Watson platform.The AI should check the names and data of the insured as well as their medical history and assess injuries.

2014 EUGENE

Eugene Goostman is a chatbot that has been developed since 2001. To make him believable for chat partners, he imitated the personality of a 13-year-old Ukrainian boy. At a Royal Society event in London organized by Kevin Warwick, Eugene Goostman managed to convince 33 percent of his human chat partners that he was a human being and not a computer. Warwick then explained that the bot had passed the Turing test. This conclusion was discussed controversially, partly due to the experimental setup.

2014 ALEXA

Amazon Echo (Alexa) is a smart speaker and voice-controlled, internet-based intelligent personal assistant from the American company Amazon. It accesses various proprietary services as well as third-party services. Alexa can perform actions such as playing music, setting an alarm or timer, explaining terms, managing calendars, etc.

2016 TAY

Tay was a Microsoft-developed artificial intelligence chatbot that went public on March 23, 2016 via Twitter. It subsequently caused public controversy when the bot started writing offensive tweets, forcing Microsoft to shut down the service just 16 hours after it started.Tay was developed by Microsoft with the goal of testing how artificial intelligence can learn in everyday life. According to Microsoft, Tay should be concerned with people and entertained. The bot should also be able to create user profiles and thus personalize communication.

AI Today

Anyone who has recently read about artificial intelligence will quickly get a bizarre impression: Today or tomorrow at the latest, we will be dealing with autonomously acting and thinking machines - be it threatening killer drones on the one hand or salvation-enhancing surgical robots on the other. And the production robots will make many of our jobs obsolete. Not to forget the artificial intelligence, which outweighs our actions in real and digital life, sometimes more or less precisely, and draws conclusions and predictions from them.

Tool Box AI

In practice, people who research with artificial intelligence have at most a tired smile for these scenarios. They are about as far from reality as a weekend trip to the next solar system. Today, artificial intelligence can be compared to a well-equipped toolbox. Just as there are screwdrivers, pipe wrenches or hammers for screwing, loosening or striking applications, there are data interpretation using AI route search engines for Google Maps, web search engines like Bing or image classification machines like Clarifai. And - in order to further weave the analogy - just as one does various activities in building a home (e.g. building something or tearing it down) and requires associated special tools and materials, there are also fundamentally different types of activities (e.g. classifying or marking) and materials in the area of ​​artificial intelligence - mostly in the form of data or data-producing sensors.

Save Life with AI

A specific AI activity is, for example, making forecasts and possible courses based on structured data. An example from medicine: If a patient suffers from a slowly falling, then suddenly rapidly rising fever after an infection, an artificial intelligence, taking into account other factors, can decide to raise the alarm immediately before the next regular human control - and save valuable hours. All data streams and signals in this context can be clearly differentiated from one another and are thus structured. All that is required is a constant monitoring of relevant signals, as well as a classification machine that learns to differentiate between dangerous and regular situations and can assess different situations better and better through continuous use.This does not replace regular inspection tours and therefore does not replace human responsibility in the hospital. But AI can provide meaningful support for staff and save one or two lives.
